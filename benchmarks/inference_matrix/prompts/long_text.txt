ARTICLE:
The proliferation of large language models (LLMs) has fundamentally reshaped the landscape of natural language processing. From conversational agents to automated programming assistants, these systems now permeate critical infrastructure across industries. Yet despite their pervasive deployment, the inference-time efficiency of LLMs remains a stubborn engineering challenge, one that determines not only operational cost but also user experience and the feasibility of on-device deployment. This article examines the principal techniques developed over the past three years to accelerate LLM inference on commodity hardware, with a particular focus on consumer-grade Apple Silicon and small-form-factor x86 systems.

We begin by characterizing the canonical inference workload. A typical autoregressive transformer must perform two distinct phases for each request: a prefill phase, in which the entire prompt is processed in a single batched forward pass, and a decode phase, in which one token at a time is produced, each conditioned on all previously generated tokens via a key-value (KV) cache. The prefill phase is compute-bound and dominated by large matrix multiplications of shape (sequence_length x hidden_dim) by (hidden_dim x hidden_dim), while the decode phase is memory-bound: each new token requires a sweep over the KV cache and a re-read of the model weights. On consumer hardware with limited memory bandwidth, the decode phase typically determines the user-perceived tokens-per-second.

Quantization has emerged as the single most consequential optimization. Modern GGUF quantizations, including Q4_K_M, IQ4_XS, IQ2_XXS, and Unsloth Dynamic variants, compress weights to between 2 and 4 bits per parameter while preserving generation quality, often within one perplexity point of the FP16 baseline. By shrinking the working set, these quantizations directly translate to higher decode throughput on bandwidth-constrained systems. Reported gains on Apple M4 Pro hardware include 1.8x to 3.2x improvements in tokens-per-second for the decode phase relative to FP16, with Q4_K_M generally offering the best quality-speed Pareto frontier and IQ2_XXS enabling 35-billion-parameter models to fit within a 24-gigabyte unified memory budget.

A second axis of optimization is kernel fusion. Hand-tuned Metal and CUDA kernels for fused attention, fused gating in mixture-of-experts (MoE) layers, and fused layer normalization have collectively eliminated tens of microseconds of per-token overhead. Flash attention variants, originally designed for training, have been adapted for inference and now form the backbone of long-context decoding. The cumulative impact of these kernels is most visible during prefill, where pp512 throughput on a Q8_0 8-billion-parameter model on an M4 Pro now exceeds 600 tokens per second when fully Metal-accelerated.

Mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures introduce both opportunities and challenges. Models such as Qwen 3.6 35B A3B activate only three billion parameters per token, dramatically reducing FLOPs while preserving model capacity. However, the expert routing pattern creates irregular memory access, and naive implementations often see decode throughput drop below dense models of comparable activated size. Recent kernels with explicit expert-batching and weight-staging have closed this gap to within 10 percent of theoretical peak.

KV cache management is the third major axis. Modern attention variants such as sliding window attention (SWA) and grouped query attention (GQA) reduce the per-token KV footprint by factors of four to eight. Rolling SWA caches in particular allow long-context generation without unbounded memory growth, but they require careful chunked-prefill logic to remain bit-equivalent with single-pass attention. Failure modes include subtle off-by-one errors in window boundaries that manifest only at very long contexts.

Looking ahead, two open questions dominate the research agenda. First, can speculative decoding deliver the promised 2-3x decode speedup on memory-bound consumer hardware, or does the bandwidth bottleneck nullify the gains? Second, how should multimodal projectors (mmproj) be co-scheduled with the language model on unified-memory systems to avoid stalling the GPU while the image or audio encoder runs on the CPU?

In conclusion, the past three years have produced a remarkably mature stack of inference-time optimizations. The cumulative effect is that 30-billion-parameter MoE models can now run interactively on a 24-gigabyte laptop, a feat that would have been considered implausible only a few years ago. Continued progress will likely come from co-design of kernels, quantization formats, and runtime scheduling rather than from any single breakthrough.
