The history of computing stretches back far longer than the silicon chips that
define the modern era, and to understand where machine intelligence is heading it
helps to remember how slowly, and how stubbornly, the idea of mechanical
calculation took shape. Long before electricity, human beings devised mechanical
aids to reckoning. The abacus, used across the ancient world from Mesopotamia to
the markets of China, let merchants add and subtract large sums with practiced
speed, encoding quantity as the position of beads on a rod. In the seventeenth
century Blaise Pascal built a geared adding machine to ease his father's
tax-collecting work, and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz extended the idea with a
stepped-drum device that could multiply and divide. Leibniz also articulated the
binary system that would, three full centuries later, become the native language
of every digital computer ever built. He dreamed of a calculus of reasoning, a
universal symbolic language in which disputes could be settled by computation
rather than rhetoric, and although the dream outran the engineering of his age,
the ambition never entirely went away.

The nineteenth century brought the first machine that deserves to be called a
computer in something like the modern sense. Charles Babbage, frustrated by the
errors that crept into the printed mathematical tables on which navigation and
engineering depended, designed the Difference Engine to compute and typeset
polynomial tables automatically. He then conceived a far more ambitious successor,
the Analytical Engine, which separated a "store" for numbers from a "mill" that
operated on them, and which was to be programmed with punched cards borrowed from
the Jacquard loom. Ada Lovelace, translating an Italian account of the machine,
appended notes longer than the original paper in which she described how the
engine might be made to weave algebraic patterns, and famously observed that it
could in principle manipulate symbols of any kind, not merely numbers. The
machine was never finished, defeated by the tolerances of Victorian metalwork and
the limits of Babbage's funding and temper, but the conceptual architecture
survived to be rediscovered a hundred years later.

The decisive theoretical leap came in 1936, when a young Alan Turing, addressing a
narrow problem in mathematical logic posed by David Hilbert, invented an abstract
machine of startling generality. Turing imagined a device that reads and writes
symbols on an infinite tape according to a finite table of rules, and showed that
a single such machine, suitably programmed, could imitate any other. This
universal machine drew a sharp line between what is computable and what is not,
proving that some well-posed questions can never be answered by any mechanical
procedure whatsoever. The same year Alonzo Church reached an equivalent
conclusion by a completely different route, through the lambda calculus, and the
agreement of the two approaches gave rise to the thesis that anything effectively
calculable can be computed by a Turing machine. That thesis, never proved but
never refuted, remains the bedrock on which the entire discipline rests, and it is
the reason a chatbot, a spreadsheet, and a flight-control system are in a deep
sense the same kind of object running different programs.

War accelerated everything. The need to break enemy ciphers and to compute
artillery firing tables turned theory into wiring with extraordinary speed. At
Bletchley Park, Turing and his colleagues built electromechanical and then
electronic machines to attack German codes, and in the United States the ENIAC,
a room-sized assembly of some eighteen thousand vacuum tubes, was wired to compute
trajectories. Reprogramming the ENIAC meant physically replugging cables and
flipping switches, a process that could take days, and it was this very
awkwardness that motivated the next great idea. In a 1945 report drafted from the
work of the ENIAC team, John von Neumann set out an architecture in which the
program itself is stored in the same memory as the data it manipulates. The
stored-program concept meant a computer could be re-tasked in seconds rather than
days, could modify its own instructions, and could be built once and used for an
unbounded variety of purposes. Almost every machine since has been, in essence, a
von Neumann machine, and the few that depart from the pattern do so deliberately
and at the margins.

The transistor, invented at Bell Labs in 1947, replaced the hot, fragile, power-
hungry vacuum tube with a sliver of semiconductor, and the integrated circuit a
decade later placed many transistors on a single chip. From these inventions flowed
the most consequential empirical regularity in the history of technology. In 1965
Gordon Moore observed that the number of transistors that could be economically
placed on a chip was doubling roughly every two years, and that exponential, which
came to bear his name, held for half a century through relentless and ferociously
expensive engineering. Each doubling made computers cheaper, faster, and smaller,
and each generation of cheaper computers found new uses that funded the next
generation. The mainframe gave way to the minicomputer, the minicomputer to the
microprocessor, and the microprocessor put a general-purpose computer first on
every desk and then in every pocket. What had been a scarce resource guarded by
white-coated operators became an ambient utility, as unremarkable and as essential
as electric light.

Software evolved in step with the hardware, climbing a ladder of abstraction that
let programmers think in ever larger units. The earliest machines were programmed
in raw numeric codes, then in assembly languages that gave mnemonic names to those
codes, and then in high-level languages such as Fortran, Lisp, and COBOL that let
a programmer write something closer to mathematics or to English and leave a
compiler to translate it down to the metal. Each layer of abstraction hid the one
beneath it, trading a little efficiency for an enormous gain in human productivity,
and the pattern repeated all the way up: operating systems hid the hardware,
libraries hid the operating system, frameworks hid the libraries, and eventually
entire applications were assembled from components whose inner workings their
authors never examined. The discipline of computer science grew up around the
problem of managing this complexity, developing data structures, algorithms, type
systems, and proofs of correctness so that programs of millions of lines could be
built by teams of people who could never hold the whole thing in a single mind.

The network changed the meaning of a computer as profoundly as the stored program
had. The ARPANET, funded in the late nineteen-sixties to connect a handful of
research institutions, pioneered packet switching, the idea of chopping every
message into small independently routed parcels that find their own way across a
mesh of links and are reassembled at the destination. This design was robust in a
way that circuit switching could never be, since there was no single path to sever
and no central exchange to fail. Out of the ARPANET grew the Internet, knit
together by the TCP/IP protocols that let heterogeneous networks interoperate as
though they were one, and on top of that substrate Tim Berners-Lee layered the
World Wide Web, a system of addressable documents linked to one another by simple
references. Suddenly a computer was not a box on a desk but a window onto a global
fabric of information and other people, and the economic and social consequences of
that shift are still working themselves out, reshaping commerce, politics, friendship,
and the very idea of a public square.

The most recent chapter, and the one whose ending no one can yet see, is the rise of
machine learning. For decades the dominant approach to artificial intelligence was
to encode human knowledge as explicit rules, an approach that worked in narrow,
well-bounded domains and shattered against the messiness of the real world. The
alternative, statistical and connectionist, was to build systems that learn their
behavior from data rather than receiving it from a programmer. Artificial neural
networks, loosely inspired by the brain, adjust the strengths of connections among
simple units so as to reduce the error of their predictions, and the algorithm of
backpropagation provides an efficient way to apportion blame for a mistake across
millions of those connections. For a long time the approach was starved of the two
things it needed most, data and computation, and it languished at the margins of the
field. The arrival of vast datasets scraped from the digitized world, and of
graphics processors whose thousands of arithmetic units turned out to be ideally
suited to the dense linear algebra of neural networks, removed both bottlenecks at
once, and the field was transformed almost overnight.

The transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, proved to be the catalyst for the
present era. Earlier networks for processing sequences read their input one element
at a time, which made them slow to train and forgetful over long distances. The
transformer replaced that sequential reading with a mechanism called attention, in
which every position in a sequence can directly consult every other position and
decide how much weight to give it. Because these comparisons can all be performed at
once, the architecture maps beautifully onto parallel hardware and scales to data and
model sizes that earlier designs could never have reached. Trained on enormous
quantities of text to predict the next token in a sequence, transformer language
models acquired, as an apparent side effect of that simple objective, a startling
breadth of competence: they translate, summarize, write code, answer questions, and
carry on conversations, and their abilities improve smoothly and predictably as they
are made larger and fed more data. The empirical scaling laws that describe this
improvement have become a kind of compass for the whole industry.

Inference in such a model divides naturally into two phases whose costs differ
sharply, and understanding the difference is the whole reason a benchmark like this
one separates them. In the first phase, often called prefill or prompt processing,
the model ingests the entire prompt at once and computes a representation of it; this
phase is richly parallel and compute-bound, since every token in the prompt can be
processed simultaneously and the arithmetic units of the accelerator can be kept
busy. In the second phase, called decoding or generation, the model emits one token
at a time, each new token depending on all the tokens before it, and this phase is
typically memory-bound, limited not by arithmetic but by the bandwidth required to
stream the model's weights and its growing cache of attention keys and values out of
memory for every single step. A serving system lives or dies by how well it handles
both regimes, overlapping the compute-heavy prefill of one request with the
memory-heavy decoding of another, batching where it can, and caching aggressively so
that a shared prefix is never processed twice. Measuring the two phases honestly, on
prompts long enough that fixed overheads no longer dominate, is the only way to know
where an engine truly spends its time, and it is the reason the prompts that follow
are deliberately, almost stubbornly, long.
